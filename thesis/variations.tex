\section{Weaknesses of the Classical Merkle--Hellman Scheme}

The classical Merkle--Hellman cryptosystem is based on a clear trapdoor idea:
an easy private superincreasing subset-sum instance is transformed into a
public instance that should look hard \cite{MerkleHellman1978}. However, in the
basic construction, this hiding transformation is not strong enough to remove
all useful structure. As a result, the public key does not behave like a fully
random hard subset-sum instance, and this opens the door to efficient
cryptanalysis. 

A major result was given by Shamir, who showed a polynomial-time
attack against the basic Merkle--Hellman scheme \cite{Shamir1982}. The attack
demonstrates that, even without knowing the private sequence directly, an
equivalent private structure can be reconstructed from public information. This
breaks the intended trapdoor asymmetry and shows that the original scheme is
insecure in its classical form. 

Another important line of attacks is connected
with low-density subset-sum instances and lattice-based methods
\cite{LagariasOdlyzko1985,Brickell1983}. In these settings, techniques related
to lattice reduction can recover message information more efficiently than
exhaustive search. Although subset-sum is NP-complete in general, practical
cryptanalytic success depends on instance structure and parameters, not only on
worst-case complexity. 

Because of this, the historical lesson from classical
Merkle--Hellman is that NP-completeness alone does not guarantee cryptographic
security. A public-key design must ensure that the published instances do not
leak exploitable structure, even if the underlying problem is hard in general
\cite{OdlyzkoRiseFall}. These weaknesses motivated many modified knapsack
proposals, discussed in the next section

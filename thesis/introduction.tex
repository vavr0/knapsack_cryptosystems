\chapter*{Introduction} 
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

Cryptography is a fundamental piece of modern information security, enabling
confidential communication, data integrity, and authentication over insecure
channels. One of the earliest public key cryptosystems is based on the
\emph{Knapsack Problem}, a fundamental problem in combinatorial optimization
and number theory.

The \textbf{Knapsack Cryptosystem}, initially proposed by Merkle and Hellman in
1978, leverages the computational difficulty of the subset sum problem to
provide security. This cryptosystem encodes messages as sums of carefully
chosen integers, making decryption without the private key computationally
infeasible. Over the years, various \emph{variations and improvements} have
been proposed to enhance security, efficiency, and resistance against
cryptanalytic attacks \cite{stanoyevitch2011}.

The aim of this thesis is to study the classical Knapsack Cryptosystem and its
variants, understand the underlying mathematical principles, implement the
algorithms, and analyze their effectiveness.
